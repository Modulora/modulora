# Modulora Brand

> Status: working direction · Updated: 2026-07-11

## Name

**Modulora**

The name is derived from “module” but remains broad enough to support React, Vue, Svelte, agents, registry infrastructure, and future distribution formats.

Pronunciation: **mod-yoo-LOR-ah**.

## Tagline

**Discover your next great component.**

The tagline leads with the user outcome: discovery. It is warm, memorable, framework-neutral, and does not burden the first impression with implementation or security terminology.

## Hero support

> Browse open and premium components from trusted creators. Install with one command—or let your coding agent handle it.

Short alternative:

> Find the right component, send it to your agent, and keep building.

“Premium” means commercially distributed by the creator; it does not imply Modulora stores or sells the source.

## Positioning

Modulora is the creator-first component registry for developers and coding agents. Creators publish from canonical sources and retain control of their work. Developers discover open and commercial components and install supported releases through a simple CLI.

### Product promise

- Better discovery than searching disconnected galleries and repositories.
- Clear creator identity and source ownership.
- One install workflow for people and coding agents.
- Free public profiles, listings, source access, and installs for open components.
- Honest treatment of commercial components and external purchase paths.
- Security evidence presented precisely without pretending scans guarantee safety.

## Brand posture

Modulora blends four qualities:

1. **Warm discovery** in headlines and onboarding.
2. **Confident clarity** in interface and product copy.
3. **Technical precision** in security, provenance, permissions, and installation.
4. **Creator-first principles** without performative rebellion or competitor attacks.

The brand is agent-native but not AI-hyped. AI is a workflow participant, not the product's personality.

## Voice attributes

### Clear

Say exactly what the user can do and what will happen.

- Prefer: “Install with one command.”
- Avoid: “Supercharge your development workflow.”

### Calmly confident

Use short declarative sentences. Do not oversell certainty.

- Prefer: “This release was scanned on July 11.”
- Avoid: “This component is completely secure.”

### Creator-respectful

Name and link the person or organization behind the work. Never imply that Modulora created community components.

- Prefer: “Created by Acme UI. Published from acme/ui.”
- Avoid: “Our Acme dashboard component.”

### Effortless

Lead with the outcome, especially when the CLI or an agent handles mechanics.

- Prefer: “Send it to your agent.”
- Avoid: “Inspect the normalized manifest and manually reconcile every source file.”

Technical evidence remains available when needed, but it does not dominate discovery copy.

### Precise about trust

Describe evidence and limits rather than awarding vague trust labels.

- Prefer: “Owner verified · Artifact signed · Scanned 2 days ago.”
- Avoid: “100% safe” or a generic “Verified” badge.

### Open and honest

Distinguish platform facts from creator claims and external services.

- Prefer: “Purchase on the creator’s website. Source not assessed by Modulora.”
- Avoid: “Secure premium install” when Modulora has not seen the code.

## Tone by context

| Context | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Homepage | Warm, concise, aspirational | “Discover your next great component.” |
| Search/results | Useful and restrained | “42 accessible date pickers.” |
| Component page | Creator-forward | “Built and maintained by Northstar UI.” |
| CLI | Direct and operational | “4 files will be added. 2 dependencies will change.” |
| Agent prompt | Compact and unambiguous | “Compare these releases and recommend one.” |
| Security evidence | Exact and sober | “Secret scan completed against digest …” |
| Warning | Calm, actionable | “This release is unsigned. Review it before installing.” |
| Error | Human and specific | “We couldn’t verify repository access. Reconnect GitHub and try again.” |
| Commercial handoff | Transparent | “You’re leaving Modulora to purchase from creator.com.” |
| Incident | Factual and accountable | “Installs of version 1.4.2 are blocked while we investigate.” |
| Social/community | Curious and generous | “What component saved you the most time this week?” |

## Writing rules

1. Lead with the user’s job, not Modulora’s architecture.
2. Use “component,” not “asset,” “content,” or “resource,” unless technically necessary.
3. Use framework-neutral brand language. React can appear in filters, compatibility, launch announcements, and technical docs.
4. Use “creator” for the person or team making components; use “developer” for the person installing them.
5. Use “coding agent” or “agent,” not “AI-powered magic.”
6. Use active voice and concrete verbs: discover, publish, install, compare, verify, revoke.
7. Keep headlines under eight words where possible.
8. Avoid exclamation points in product UI.
9. Avoid fear-based security copy. State the condition and action.
10. Never use “verified” without naming what was verified.
11. Never call source “open source” without a recognized license.
12. Never imply payment improves trust, rank, or moderation outcomes.
13. Never attack competitors or repeat unsubstantiated allegations.

## Vocabulary

### Prefer

- Discover
- Creator
- Component
- Publish
- Install
- Release
- Source
- Evidence
- Open source
- Commercial
- Purchase on creator site
- Owner verified
- Artifact signed
- Scanned
- Revoked
- Send to your agent

### Avoid

- Magic
- Revolutionary
- Game-changing
- AI-powered (unless technically distinguishing a feature)
- Premium quality (price does not prove quality)
- Safe/secure component (without qualification)
- Certified (unless backed by a defined certification)
- Our component (for creator-owned work)
- Steal, stolen, ripped, jacked (in public brand communications)
- Community Edition

## Core message hierarchy

### 1. Discovery

> Discover your next great component.

### 2. Ease

> Install with one command—or let your coding agent handle it.

### 3. Creator trust

> Published by the people who made it.

### 4. Openness and choice

> Open components are free to inspect and install. Commercial components stay under creator control.

### 5. Security

> Every release carries clear, scoped evidence—not vague promises.

Security is essential to the product but should generally appear after discovery, ease, and creator ownership in marketing. In installation and account flows, security becomes primary.

### Hero

**Discover your next great component.**

Browse open and premium components from trusted creators. Install with one command—or let your coding agent handle it.

**Primary CTA:** Explore components  
**Secondary CTA:** Publish a component

### Creator section

## Built by creators. Kept in their control.

Publish from your canonical source, keep your profile and public listings free, and choose how people access your work.

### Agent section

## Find it here. Ship it with your agent.

Search Modulora from your browser, terminal, or coding agent. Every path resolves to the same versioned component release.

### Trust section

## Trust the evidence, not a badge.

See who published a release, where it came from, and which checks ran—without pretending a scan guarantees safety.

### Commercial section

## Open or commercial. Always creator-led.

Open-source components remain free to browse and install. Commercial components send you to the creator’s verified purchase page.

> Commercial component

> Sponsored  
> This creator paid for placement. Sponsorship does not affect trust evidence or organic rank.

> Add `@northstar/calendar@1.2.0`?  
> 4 files will be added and 2 dependencies will change.

### Security warning

> This release is unsigned. Automatic installation is blocked by your current policy.

## Brand review checklist

Before copy ships, ask:

- Is the creator clearly credited?
- Is the statement framework-neutral unless context requires otherwise?
- Does the copy lead with an outcome?
- Are security claims scoped and evidenced?
- Is a creator claim distinguishable from a Modulora fact?
- Is commercial/external fulfillment unmistakable?
- Could the same meaning be expressed with fewer words?
- Does it sound calm rather than hyped or defensive?
